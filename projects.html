Research Projects

  • quantum ames
    Jan 2020- May 2020

    Currently, in the Fall of 2020, I am doing a research project at Ames National Laboratory with ECE Professor and Associate Scientist Durga Paudyal on quantum computers. Since I had spent the summer building my technical knowledge in quantum mechanics and quantum computing, I felt more comfortable tackling a project in this field. I chose the direction of this project to be quantum machine-learning since I am most interested in studying powerful applications of quantum circuit design. I extensively researched the many ways quantum algorithms provide new power to machine-learning and found the topic of quantum neural networks to have many exciting possibilities. My work is now about creating evaluation metrics and implementations of parametrized quantum circuits as neurons inside of these hybrid quantum-classical networks. We are working with the intention of publishing a paper by December this year

  • kothari / HON490
    Aug 2019- Dec 2019

    During the Fall of 2019, I began my University Honors capstone project with ECE Professor Suraj Kothari. I met Dr. Kothari during the previous summer, and he agreed to have me as a student after we discussed our similarity in studying mathematics as a starting point for learning about computer science. We worked with a software analysis tool called Atlas that transformed software into directed graphs for the sake of human comprehension. My research initially began with extending the analysis platform to be compatible with Python by exploring its compiler, but the project focus shifted as Kothari was making more progress with his own research. We ended up transitioning to studying a paper that described a path counting algorithm that we would implement into Atlas. Initially, we turned the directed control flow graphs into systems of linear equations to solve, but Dr. Kothari invented an ingenious way of transforming the graphs into regular expressions used for path counting at an improved time complexity. Following Dr. With Kothari's work, I was able to implement the solution into Atlas. This experience prepared me for graduate research because I learned about integrating my own abilities into an ongoing research endeavor.

  • Ames National Laboratory Cyber Security Internship
    May 2019- Aug 2019

    I spent the summer of 2019 at Ames National Laboratory working with Cyber Security Research Manager Grant Johnson on a machine-learning application for software security evaluation. After analyzing the current state of existing analysis tools, we decided to use the National Vulnerability Database to find GitHub commits which patch the vulnerability identified in the database entry. I then designed an algorithm to find the commit most likely to have contributed to the vulnerability associated with the patch commit. This algorithm then populated a dataset of commits. Using the commit message, the commit author’s number of previous commits in the repository, and lines of code changed, we performed machine-learning to classify the likelihood that any commit may have contributed a vulnerability. I learned about machinelearning principal component analysis and accuracy validation during this project. Our model ended up outperforming a similar production model in precision and recall rate with 82.80% accuracy, 75% recall, and 85.22% precision. This project was a major step in preparing me for graduate school because I was personally responsible for the major directions of the project based on my prior skills, background knowledge, and initial study of the field.

  • Machine Learning Assisted Monte-Carlo Simulations / HON290
    Jan 2018 - May 2018
    Click here to view final poster.

    In the Spring of 2018, I participated in the Freshman Honors Research program that paired me with Physics Professor Peter Orth. I taught myself Python to create a Monte Carlo simulation of an Ising Model, a mathematical model of ferromagnetic materials. We studied machine-learning algorithms to cluster the spins to flip orientation in a lattice at each time step. I struggled with this project because I was a beginner with the coding language and was not familiar with machine-learning yet. The completed project included the design of a poster that displayed physical observables of our model which verified the model’s correctness. We also included the statistical significance of the difference in magnetization between Monte Carlo steps to structure our learning algorithm rate, as well as calculations of standard error for different lattice sizes. While my coding contributions to this project were done individually, I was mentored by Dr. Orth and his postdoc student. Together, they helped me debug my work and better understand the material. This experience taught me about using literature as a starting point of a project, and I enjoyed it to the point I would continue to search for more research opportunities.
